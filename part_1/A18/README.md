# A18 – AI Hallucination Cases in Generative AI

## Definition

A hallucination in generative artificial intelligence refers to a situation where an AI system produces false or fabricated information while presenting it as factual. This is particularly risky in fields such as law where accuracy is essential.

---

## Case 1: Oberoi v Douglas (2026)

In *Oberoi v Douglas (2026)*, the court identified the inappropriate use of generative AI in legal proceedings. The applicant submitted legal materials containing **non-existent case citations and fabricated quotations**.

The judgment highlighted that the use of AI resulted in hallucinated legal authorities, meaning that some cited cases were incorrect or did not exist. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}  

The court also noted that generative AI produces outputs based on probability rather than verified facts, and therefore should not be relied upon without proper validation. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}  

---

## Case 2: Morcos v Bayside Council (2026)

In *Morcos v Bayside Council (2026)*, the Commission identified another instance of AI hallucination. The applicant submitted a list of legal authorities, some of which were found to be **hallucinated and non-existent cases**.

The Commission explicitly noted the presence of hallucinated cases in the submitted materials, raising concerns about the use of AI in legal document preparation. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}  

This case demonstrates how unverified AI-generated content can mislead legal proceedings.

---

## Conclusion

Both cases demonstrate that generative AI systems can produce hallucinated outputs, including fabricated legal cases and incorrect citations.

These examples highlight the importance of:

- Verifying AI-generated content  
- Maintaining professional responsibility  
- Avoiding reliance on AI without validation
